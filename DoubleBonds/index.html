Double Bonds and Beyond

Double, triple, and beyond contains molecules where bonds are formed when atoms are sharing pairs of electrons, but these are more complex than the simple bonds. In some of these molecules double or triple covalent bonds are created to achieve a full valence shell.

Each oxygen atom has 6 valence electrons. To have a full valence shell of 8 electrons, oxygen shares two electrons from each atom. This results in a total of 4 electrons shared as two pairs between the two nuclei forming a double covalent bond.
Oxygen has 6 valence electrons but to fill the valence shell it must share 2 electrons. Carbon has 4 valence electrons. For interesting but complex reasons, carbon shares each of these with other atoms. In the case of CO2, carbon shares two electrons with each oxygen making a total of 4 electrons on each side of the carbon atom. This molecule is linear to evenly spread out the negative charge from the bonds and the remaining non-bonding electrons on the two oxygen atoms. Be sure to rotate this molecule around to see it from all angles.
Nitrogen atoms have 5 valence electrons each. They will be stable with a full valence shell of 8 electrons so must share 3 electrons each. The 3 pairs of electrons between the atoms result in a very strong, short triple bond and an immensely stable molecule.

Ammonia and methane are in this section not because of their single bonds but because of their shapes. Both form multiple single bonds but on paper the true molecular shape is hard to visualize. Rotate these molecules to see the pyramidal and tetrahedral shapes they form.
