Reaction Kinetics

Reactions take place when reactant particles collide with each other. There are several methods that can be used to control the way these collisions take place and the resulting speed of reaction. A few of the key controlling methods are explained in the experiences below. The only one missing is pressure which applies only to gases.

For any reaction to take place the reactant particles must collide with the right amount of force and with the particles in the correct orientation. The example shown here is a general example, but the concepts apply to all reactions. Controlling the speed and number of these collisions in turn, controls the rate of the reaction.
Changing the temperature of of reactions works for fluids (liquids, solutions, and gases). Adding heat energy results in an increase in kinetic energy of the particles. If the particles are moving more and moving faster, there is a greater chance of a collision, so the rate of reaction will increase. There are some exceptions to this, but the majority of reactions speed up when they are warmed. In this temperature change experience, you can see stylized particles reacting in a warm and cold environment. Watch to see which reaction finishes first.
Changing concentration applies to liquids and solutions. An increase in concentration means there are more reactant particles present in the reaction vessel. If there are more particles present the chances of a collision resulting in a reaction are increased. In this concentration experience you can see stylized particles reacting in both a high and low concentration solution. Watch to see which reaction finishes first.
By grinding up a solid into smaller pieces, the surface area increases. The same mass and number of particles are present in both situations but because more of them are exposed in the smaller pieces, more collisions can take place and so the reaction speeds up.
Catalysts reduce the activation energy (the energy required to get a reaction started). Catalysts provide an alternative, lower energy pathway for the reaction to occur and causes it to speed up. Catalysts tend to be specific to a reaction and inorganic catalysts are often from the transition metal section of the periodic table. One key thing about inorganic catalysts is that they are not used up in the reaction as they are not a reactant; they can be reused again and again.
